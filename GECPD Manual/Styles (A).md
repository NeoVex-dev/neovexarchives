\# GECPD Manual

\# Styles (A)

\# *Published Date: 12/06/2026.*



Hey there, I recently created my own game documentation method (The GECPD - Game Experience Cinematics \& Progression Document), and thought I should make a manual for it. Part A of the styles section of the GECPD manual covers intro scenes/cutscenes, but really this approach is used for most cinematic descriptions in the GECPD. The intro and cutscene sections of your document should ideally be the most descriptive and detailed parts in the document.



Anyways, in this section of the manual, I'm going to cover the different "styles" you can write the GECPD to fit your comfort zone, needs and what fits your game. Generally I would suggest to aim for the "Experience Oriented" approach, as it covers basic visual \& sound info, while focusing on what the player feels/experiences, including what the player might think or how the player may react. The purpose of these styles isn't to prescribe a single correct way to write the GECPD, but to demonstrate different levels of detail and focus.



#### **Intro/Cutscenes**

***(The following styles are examples of cutscenes/intro scenes)***



###### **Explicit Description**



&#x09;Style 1 (explicit visual description alone)



The screen fades into (animated) grain on a black screen, and the grain reduces in intensity, revealing a low-poly texture for the ground. The ball eventually appears, and the screen becomes fuzzy/blurry. The ball gains a slight rim glow as the players' view becomes sharper....







&#x09;Style 2 (explicit audio description alone) \[- Highly unrecommended]



A low bass tone plays and fades with the screen, filled with grain. The tone softens and transitions into a middle-octave mysterious score. Once the ball appears, a ringing high-pitched tone plays...







&#x09;Style 3 (general explicit description alone)



A low bass tone plays while the screen fades into (animated) grain on a black screen. The grain reduces in intensity \[tone softens], revealing a low-poly texture for the ground \[middle-octave mysterious score]. The ball eventually appears, and the screen becomes fuzzy/blurry while a ringing high-pitched tone plays. The ball gains a slight rim glow as the players' view becomes sharper....







###### **Experience Oriented**



&#x09;Style 1 (general)



A low bass tone plays while the screen fades into (animated) grain on a black screen. The grain reduces in intensity \[tone softens], revealing a low-poly texture for the ground \[middle-octave mysterious score]. The player gains an understanding of what the environment might look like. The ball eventually appears, and the screen becomes fuzzy/blurry while a ringing high-pitched tone plays. The ball gains a slight rim glow as the players' view becomes sharper. The controls are handed over to the player, who is likely confused about how movement functions in the game.







&#x09;Style 2 (polished + feeling focused + developer notes)

A low bass tone plays invoking a sense of confusion while the screen fades into (animated) grain on a black screen. The grain reduces in intensity \[tone softens]. The ground is revealed, the player notices the low poly texture, and understands the game environment \[middle-octave mysterious score]. The ball eventually appears, and the screen becomes fuzzy/blurry while a ringing high-pitched tone plays. This scene in particular is the first one to immerse the player into the character, giving the player the feeling of gaining/regaining consciousness. The ball gains a slight rim glow as the players' view becomes sharper. The controls are handed over to the player, who is likely confused about how movement functions in the game.



Notice how, in all the styles above, some changes are significant while others are subtle. Each approach changes the developer's perspective of the game and encourages different ways of thinking about the player's experience. I would also like to emphasize how the "Experience Oriented" styles not only explicitly declare what the player is supposed to think, but forces the developer to put themselves into the shoes of the player and write down what parts the player may not understand. Although I generally recommend the Experience Oriented styles, you should ultimately choose the one that best fits your workflow.
