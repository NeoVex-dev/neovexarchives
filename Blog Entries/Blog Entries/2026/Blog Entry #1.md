Mars is harsh, and a mission to colonize it is filled with opportunities for failure, but hey, we have to at least give it a chance...

Before we dive into the possibilities of colonizing mars, we should probably consider why we would want to colonize another planet in the first place. Let's start with why mars and not another planet.



**Why Mars?**



Mars is considered one of the most practical targets for human colonization because it possesses several resources that could support human life. Apart from that Mars contains raw materials required to support human survival (without the right equipment, however, it remains hostile). For example, Mars consists of subsurface-ice that can be extracted and purified into drinking water. Its atmosphere is composed almost entirely of Carbon Dioxide, which can be processed and turned into oxygen. These factors make Mars the most likely candidate for a long-term colony outside of our planet.





**Colonizing Mars**



I've got to be straight, colonizing mars isn't about the earth exploding so we can migrate to mars. So before I continue I need you to clear the thought of mars being a "backup" planet, and rather think of it as a "support" planet. This is because if Earth truly ends in the distant future, finding refuge in mars would be highly impractical due to the fact that manufacturing and collecting the vital resources required to sustain the human population on mars is not only very expensive but simply not possible with today's technology.



This series of articles is based around a crew consisting of astronauts and scientists rather than the general population. So why would people want to colonize mars? Many reasons but in this series, the colony was designed in order to develop an understanding of how future colonies can evolve and what life on mars would look like. In simple terms, if the colony I design were to become a real mission, it would solely be for research and development on mars and to collect data for future missions. The purpose of colonies in general is based on the goals of the mission, but is usually either for preparation for a future colony, conducting research or actually supporting long-term life on mars for a small group.





**The Challenge**



The first step in designing a mars colony is understanding the challenges it comes with. Let's go through a few of them:

Extreme cold

Low gravity

Fine dust storms

Low atmospheric pressure

Lack of fresh water

High radiation

No native vegetation

Far from Earth

And these are only a few of the many challenges a Martian colony would face.



Understanding these challenges is only half the battle, next we must further understand and keep in mind the basic factors that affect our colony's design.





**Basic factors**



Before directly moving onto design factors, we should review some basic factors that significantly influence the habitat condition and sustainability. 



**Location** - *Influences use of solar panels and the colony's access to water.*

**Structure \& Exterior** - *Manages insulation and radiation protection, while ensuring the colonists feel both safe and happy with the environment.*

**Layout** - *Due to the cost of transporting cargo to Mars, resources are limited. Facilities must therefore be designed efficiently while still ensuring the safety of the colonists.*

**Greenhouses** - *Maintaining a safe environment for plant life.*

**Ecosystem** - *Ensuring all parts and systems in the colony work together seamlessly.*

**Redundancy** - *Keeping a secondary source for a primary source. Keeping a backup for the secondary source. Having a last emergency source if necessary. Although redundancy may not always be a good thing, in this situation it is essential to ensure safety of the colonists.*

**A few more things** - *There are a few more vital factors to keep in mind when designing a colony, as we will see in the future.*
